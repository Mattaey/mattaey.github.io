Victory for Civil Rights in Tennessee

September 17, 2019

Many of my friends and family who read this might not be from Tennessee, yet you probably know that I am. If you’ve ever had the displeasure of having to hear me talk about the current condition of politics here, I’m sorry. For me, it has been an extremely disappointing time, with our corrupt speaker Glenn Casada resining, a house member accused of sexual assault (David Byrd), and current city commissioner and candidate for mayor of Memphis, Tami Sawyer, having her vile tweets dug up days before early-voting started. 

 

Despite all of that negativity, what was still affecting me the most was a law passed earlier this year. Back in May, our governor signed the Third-Party Voter Registration Law (HB 1079), which would’ve been effective October of this year. A first of its kind in the country, it targeted voter registration groups and drives by implementing fines and criminal penalties for numerous new requirements. In a deeply red state, it isn’t hard to connect the dots. Last year, the Tennessee Black Voter Project managed to register almost 100,000 voters, and there were complaints that they didn’t submit them with enough time, or with too many errors. 



This past week, throughout the madness that is six college classes  and LSAT prep, was the news that the NAACP’s and ACLU’s lawsuit to stop this bill was successful. It was an incredible victory for civil rights across the state of Tennessee, and I was grinning as soon as I read the email. I instantly forwarded to anyone I thought was interested, and even to some who probably weren’t. The federal judge who refused to dismiss the suit, said that the state should pursue other efforts to improve the faults in voter registration than those the bill set forth. 

 

What was mentioned in the opinion, which can be found here if you’re interested https://lawyerscommittee.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/PI-MEMORANDUM.pdf , was something I think a lot of people might not understand at first. These organizations are targeting groups in urban areas, such as college students and low income voters. If a law like this were to pass, thousands of the voices in the Memphis community wouldn’t be heard each election cycle.
